4.0 out of 5 starsA Comprhensive Balanced Story of USA's largest bank failure
June 13, 2013
Format: Kindle EditionVerified Purchase
In readable prose the author explains the workings of a the bank and mistakes that were made repeatedly at all levels. Huge amounts of fraud in the mortgage business were documented. At the end I was left with uncertainty whether this bank with all its bad and fraudulent mortgages needed to fail. It had at the end still respectable capital reserves despite two bank runs. The goverments seizure of banks lacks consistency. One is left with the question did the FDIC in its end rush to close it make WaMu fail when there was still a realistic chance of salvaging this bank without the government taking it over in a shot gun marriage for WaMu to JP Morgan Chase

Todd L. Margo
4.0 out of 5 starsVery good, insightful and educational
May 21, 2013
Format: HardcoverVerified Purchase
Understanding WAMU's role and demise during the 2009 financial crisis helps fill in gaps from other analyses of the crisis. In particular, this book gives insight into why its been difficult to prosecute individuals. There is enough plausible deniability on the part of at least some executives and enough complicity by the government to make significant prosecutions work or be in the least bit meaningful when so-called "settlements" are reached. For me, the book could have spent more time on analysis and a bit less on the figures involved, but that's a nit and may reflect an inability to obtain additional documents at the time of the writing.

B. S. Lunde
4.0 out of 5 starsA Fascinating Account of How Greed Hurt Us All
May 25, 2013
Format: Kindle EditionVerified Purchase
WaMu was a principal player in the financial crisis of 2008/2009. It was riding the wave of irresponsible mortgage lending that both fueled, and depended on, the housing bubble that we are still recovering from. By repackaging these absurdly risky loans into new complex debt securities that were in turn sold to investors, WaMu and other financial firms fed the financial markets with time-delayed poison. The whole house of cards was built on the drug of easy money and a refusal to look at the risks. This book takes us by the hand and walks us down the path with WaMu from its humble, folksy roots as a community bank to its transformation into a voracious mortgage-backed monster. Ms. Grind tells the story like the reporter she is, not as a novelist, so the book reads a bit dry, even as she does a good job putting it all in laymen's terms. A great read for anyone who wants to understand what really happened that brought on The Great Recession that inflicted so much pain on millions of Americans.

5.0 out of 5 starsWay better than The Big Short
August 20, 2012
Format: HardcoverVerified Purchase
This is, IMHO, the best book written to date about the recent (2007-2008) financial crisis. *Much* finer writing than The Big Short. Rather than placing the blame solely on the bond rating agencies, this story offers a much broader perspective. Complicated financial concepts (asset back securities, CDOs, etc.) are explained in such a pleasurable way and in the context of a suspenseful story. Can't believe I found learning about the different regulatory agencies 'thrilling'. Sure, I'm a big nerd, but this book is the bomb!

November 5, 2014

This is a great read on how the financial meltdown started.
